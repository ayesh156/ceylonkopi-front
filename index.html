Ceylon Coffee Resurgence

From 1780 to Modern Mastery

Ceylon coffee has a rich history dating back to the 1780s when it was started by the Dutch and then the British took it over. The cultivation began in the central hills, laying the foundation for the coffee industry. During the British colonial period, Ceylon coffee gained prominence. Large-scale plantations were established, and the industry flourished, contributing significantly to the country's economy.

In the 20th century, Ceylon coffee experienced a resurgence. Small-scale farmers began cultivating coffee, diversifying into different varieties, and exploring the unique flavors of the region. The late 20th century and early 21st century witnessed a global rise in the demand for specialty coffee. Ceylon coffee producers embraced this trend, focusing on quality, unique profiles, and sustainable practices. Today, Ceylon coffee has evolved into a symbol of modern mastery. Producers leverage advanced cultivation methods, sustainable practices, and a commitment to quality, positioning Ceylon coffee as a premium and sought-after choice in the global coffee market.

A Rich Heritage in Every Sip

From Colonial Elegance
to Modern Revival

Ceylon coffee, tracing its roots to the 1780s in the lush landscapes of Sri Lanka, boasts a captivating journey through history. Introduced by the Dutch, the industry flourished in the island's elevated regions, gaining recognition for its unique terroir. By the 19th century, Ceylon coffee emerged as a global sensation, renowned for its exquisite quality and distinct flavor profile. However, the industry faced setbacks in the form of devastating diseases, leading to a decline in production. In the modern era, a remarkable resurgence has occurred, with a renewed focus on specialty coffee. Ceylon coffee now delights connoisseurs with its bright acidity, medium body, and nuanced flavors, reaffirming its status as a premier coffee origin with a rich and enduring legacy.
